About Us

Welcome to the Clare McCabe research group website. Professor McCabe is a Professor in the Department of Chemical Engineering and Chemistry, Associate Dean of the Graduate School, and Director of the Office of Postdoctoral Affairs at Vanderbilt University in Nashville, TN. McCabe also directs the Multiscale Modeling and Simulation (MuMS) group at Vanderbilt, an interdisciplinary research housing faculty from the School of Engineering, and works in Vanderbilt's Institute for Nanoscale Science and Engineering (VINSE) group. Her research interests include molecular simulation, theoretical chemistry, computational structural biochemistry, chemical biology, and biophysical chemistry. ...

Since joining the Vanderbilt faculty in 2004, McCabe has displayed excellence as a mentor and supervisor for students completing their undergraduate research projects. At a 2011 faculty awards ceremony, Chancellor Nicholas S. Zeppos presented her with the Madison Sarratt Prize for Excellence in Undergraduate Teaching. McCabe also directs a Graduate Assistance in Areas of National Need (GAANN) program at Vanderbilt University in distributing funds from a U.S. Department of Education grant to fund graduate fellowships in the area of advanced materials. Throughout the years, McCabe has published in various notable journals, including the American Chemical Society, American Physical Society, CRC press, and The Royal Society of Chemistry.

Why Molecular Simulations?

Speed

Minutes to hours of server time versus hours to days of laboratory experiments. Plus, the simulation can be rewound, fastforward, and replayed at any time after completion with dynamic visualization on a user-friendly, multi-platform interface.

Cost

Budgets can only stretch so far and a pure biological compound such as cholesterol costs roughly $45/gram. In facility computer, several thousands of those molecules can be simulated for only a few cents for every hour of power/bandwidth.

Availability

Record your simulation experiment and share with any collaborator over email, google drive, or whatever. In a different country or continent but still want to run your simulation? SSH into your computer and overcome that physical barrier!

Molecular simulation and computational quantum chemistry has an increasingly important role with drug research and higher education; a distant future of 2020 hopes to bring the chemical industry the capacity to design both chemical products and manufacturing processes using molecular simulations. The Multiscale Modeling and Simulation Institute at Vanderbilt (MUMS) strives to achieve these goals. Associated articles and pages are linked to the gallery images.

Current Projects

Click on the titles to view the associated Github repositories or webpages.

Title Description
Molecular Modeling of Lipid Self-Assembly We are interested in understanding how and why skin lipid molecules assemble into the structures observed through microscopy and biophysical measurements. We hope to elucidate the role of normal and abnormal lipid composition on lipid organization and skin barrier function; the resultant conclusions will provide the molecular basis for breaching the skin barrier in a controlled manner to facilitate delivery of drugs across the skin and to develop treatment strategies that restore barrier function in diseased skin.
Development and Application of Molecular Theories The ability to accurately predict the thermodynamic properties of fluids is central to chemical product and process design. Our work focuses on the development and application of molecular based approaches to determine the thermodynamic properties and phase behavior of a wide range of fluids such as hydrocarbons, polymers, ionic liquids and electrolytes.
Improving the Efficiency of BioFuel Conversion While biofuels remain a very promising component of the solution to meeting 21st-century energy needs, low efficiencies and high cost limit the potential growth of the energy source. We focus on developing models and tools to understand the biological depolymerization of cellulose by cellulases; our ultimate goal is providing molecular level insight to enable the design of more efficient and active cellulases.
Molecular Modeling of Nanoscale Systems Since experimental design in nanoscale systems are often difficult due to the time and length scales involved, molecular modeling helps overcome these research barriers. In particular, we are interested in the study of nanoparticles, such as carbon nanotubes and polyhedral oligomeric silsesquioxanes (or more simply POSS molecules). We use molecular modeling to understand the properties for nanoparticles and composite materials are impacted by their chemical structure and composition.
